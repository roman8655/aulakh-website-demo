Anxiety Disorders

Reaching out is a courageous first step. With the right support, it's possible to reduce anxiety and move forward feeling more steady, empowered, and at peace.

If you find yourself struggling with feelings of anxiety, please know that you're not alone. Many people experience anxiety disorders, which can influence how we think, feel, and respond to stress. While anxiety is a normal — and sometimes helpful — human emotion, anxiety disorders involve excessive or persistent fear and worry that can interfere with daily life, relationships, work, and overall well-being.

The good news is that struggles with anxiety are highly treatable!

Common Types of Anxiety Disorders

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Characterized by ongoing, excessive worry about everyday concerns such as work, health, finances, or family. This constant mental strain can feel exhausting and is often accompanied by restlessness, muscle tension, and difficulty relaxing.

Panic Disorder

Involves recurrent, unexpected panic attacks — intense surges of fear that peak quickly and may include physical symptoms such as heart palpitations, chest tightness, shortness of breath, dizziness, or a sense of losing control.

Social Anxiety Disorder

Marked by a strong fear of being judged, embarrassed, or negatively evaluated in social or performance situations. This can lead to avoidance of gatherings, speaking up, or engaging in situations that feel exposing or distressing.

Specific Phobias

Persistent and disproportionate fears related to specific objects or situations — such as flying, heights, needles, or certain animals. These fears can significantly limit daily activities and feel difficult to manage without support.

Common Symptoms of Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety can affect both the mind and body. Common symptoms include the following.

  • Persistent worry or fear that feels difficult to control.
  • Feeling constantly on edge, keyed up, or restless.
  • Rapid heartbeat, shortness of breath, or sweating during anxious moments.
  • Sleep disturbances, including difficulty falling or staying asleep.
  • Trouble concentrating or focusing.
  • Avoidance of situations that trigger anxiety.

Causes and Risk Factors

Anxiety disorders are complex and often develop from a combination of biological, psychological, and environmental factors. Family history, brain chemistry, temperament, trauma, chronic stress, and significant life changes can all contribute.

It is important to remember that experiencing anxiety is not a personal failing. These challenges are part of the human experience, and seeking support is a courageous and empowering step.

The good news is that regardless of the cause, anxiety disorders are highly treatable. You also don't have to navigate this journey alone. Support is available, and meaningful change is possible.

Sessions 1-2

Sessions 3-4

What is Evidence-Based Practice?

Evidence-Based Practice is our commitment to providing care that is grounded in science, guided by clinical expertise, and tailored to you. This approach integrates the best available research with the knowledge and experience of your clinician, while also considering your unique strengths, culture, values, and personal preferences.

Evidence-based practice is more than using specific techniques. It is a thoughtful, collaborative decision- making process. Together, we review what research suggests is effective, apply professional judgment and experience, and adapt treatment to fit your individual needs. This approach extends beyond therapy methods to include assessment, case formulation, and the therapeutic relationship itself.

We also believe in ongoing monitoring and open communication. Your progress is regularly reviewed, and your treatment plan is adjusted as needed to ensure you are receiving the most effective, meaningful, and responsive care possible.

Our clinicians use research-supported approaches that are also responsive to your individual needs.

CBT is a practical, evidence-based approach that helps people understand how their thoughts, emotions, and behaviors influence one another. In CBT, clients learn to identify unhelpful thinking patterns, develop healthier perspectives, and build effective coping strategies. This collaborative, goal-focused therapy supports individuals in making meaningful changes that improve emotional well-being and everyday functioning.

ACT helps individuals develop a healthier relationship with their thoughts and emotions rather than struggling to control or avoid them. Through mindfulness and values-based strategies, ACT supports people in accepting difficult experiences while committing to actions that align with what matters most to them. This approach encourages psychological flexibility, helping individuals live more fully and meaningfully even in the presence of life's challenges.

Mindfulness-Based Approaches help individuals develop greater awareness of their thoughts, emotions, and bodily sensations in the present moment. Through practices such as mindful breathing, reflection, and gentle observation, clients learn to respond to experiences with greater clarity, openness, and self-compassion. These approaches can help reduce stress, improve emotional regulation, and support a more balanced and grounded way of relating to life's challenges.

Psychodynamic Psychotherapy focuses on understanding how past experiences, relationships, and unconscious patterns shape present thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. Through reflective conversation and a supportive therapeutic relationship, individuals can gain deeper insight into recurring emotional patterns and relational dynamics. This process helps foster greater self-awareness, emotional growth, and lasting change.

EFT helps individuals better understand, experience, and work through their emotions in a safe and supportive environment. By exploring emotional patterns and learning to respond to feelings with greater awareness and compassion, clients can transform difficult emotions and develop healthier ways of relating to themselves and others. This approach supports emotional healing, stronger relationships, and deeper self-understanding.

ABT focuses on understanding and healing patterns in relationships that originate from early life experiences. By exploring attachment styles and emotional bonds, this approach helps individuals build healthier connections, improve emotional regulation, and foster a stronger sense of trust and security in their relationships.
