Trauma

Trauma-related disorders are highly treatable, and recovery is possible.

As psychologists and social workers, our goal is to provide compassionate, structured, and evidence-based care that helps you regain stability, confidence, and hope.

Main Trauma Symptoms and Behaviors

Trauma can present in different ways, but common signs include the following.
Intrusive Memories
  • Experiencing flashbacks.
  • Feeling as if the event is happening again.
  • Nightmares.
  • Desturbing recurring memories.
Physical Reactions
  • Rapid heartbeat or sweating when reminded of the event.
Avoidance Behaviors
  • Staying away from people, places, or objects that trigger memories of the trauma.
  • Avoiding thoughts or feelings associated with the event.
Cognition and Mood Changes
  • Persistent negative beliefs about oneself or the world (e.g. "I am broken", "The world is unsafe").
  • Feelings of detachment, numbness or inability to feel joy.
  • Persistent fear, anger, guilt, or shame.
  • Loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities.
Arousal and Reactivity
  • Being easily startled or frightened.
  • Constant feelings of being "on guard" or tense.
  • Irritability, outbursts of anger, or aggressive behavior.
  • Difficulty concentrating.
  • Insomnia or sleep disruption.
Physical Symptoms
  • Chronic pain, headaches, or stomach issues.
  • Feeling dizzy or lighthearted.

Theraputic Process

Sessions 1-2

We begin by understanding your unique experience — the specific symptoms of trauma, how long they've been present, and how they're impacting your life.

Sessions 3-4

Sessions 5+

Subsequent sessions are then set at an agreed-upon frequency based on needs.

What is Evidence-Based Practice?

Our clinicians use a variety of evidence-based treatments to address trauma symptoms. These include Trauma-Focused Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (TF-CBT), Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) for Trauma, Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing Therapy (EMDR), and Internal Family Systems Therapy (IFS).

Learn more about the different types of treatment below.

TF-CBT is an evidence-based treatment designed to help individuals heal from the effects of trauma. It is widely used to support people across the lifespan who have experienced events such as abuse, violence, grief, accidents, medical trauma, bullying, or other overwhelming experiences.

Trauma can affect emotions, behaviour, relationships, sleep, concentration, and a person's sense of safety. TF-CBT provides a structured, compassionate approach that helps individuals process what happened, develop coping skills, and regain a sense of stability and confidence. A strong body of research has shown TF-CBT to be effective in reducing trauma symptoms, anxiety, depression, and behavioural difficulties.

How TF-CBT Helps

TF-CBT can help individuals:

  • Learn relaxation and grounding strategies.
  • Understand trauma responses and common reactions.
  • Identify and reframe unhelpful thoughts.
  • Gradually process traumatic memories in a safe, supported way.
  • Strengthen communication between child and caregiver.
  • Enhance safety, resilience, and future coping.

A Trauma-Informed Supportive Approach

TF-CBT is delivered at a pace that prioritizes emotional safety, trust, and collaboration. Children and families are never forced to discuss painful experiences before they are ready. Therapy focuses on creating stability first, then supporting healing in manageable steps.

With the right support, recovery is possible. TF-CBT can help individuals move from surviving trauma toward healing, connection, and renewed confidence.

CPT is an evidence-based treatment for post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and trauma-related difficulties. Originally developed for survivors of trauma, CPT is now widely used to help adults who have experienced events such as abuse, assault, accidents, military trauma, childhood adversity, grief, or other overwhelming experiences.

Trauma can deeply affect how people view themselves, others, and the world. Many individuals experience persistent beliefs such as “I am not safe,” “It was my fault,” or “I can't trust anyone.” CPT helps people understand how trauma has shaped these beliefs and supports the development of more balanced, compassionate, and accurate perspectives.

A strong body of research has shown CPT to be effective in reducing PTSD symptoms, depression, anxiety, shame, guilt, and trauma-related distress.

How CPT Helps

CPT is a structured therapy that focuses on the connection between thoughts, emotions, and behaviours. Treatment may include:

  • Understanding common trauma responses.
  • Identifying “stuck points” that block healing.
  • Challenging self-blame, shame, or overgeneralized beliefs.
  • Developing more balanced ways of thinking.
  • Processing the meaning of the traumatic event.
  • Reducing avoidance and emotional distress.
  • Rebuilding trust, safety, control, and self-esteem.

A Respectful, Collaborative, and Structured Approach

CPT is delivered in a supportive, paced, and collaborative way. Therapy does not require reliving trauma unnecessarily. Instead, it helps individuals make sense of what happened, reduce the ongoing impact of trauma, and move forward with greater clarity and confidence.

Healing often involves changing the meaning that trauma has carried. CPT offers a research-supported path toward recovery, resilience, and renewed perspective.

Trauma can leave people feeling stuck in patterns of fear, avoidance, self-criticism, emotional numbness, or disconnection from what matters most. ACT is an evidence-based approach that helps individuals build a different relationship with painful thoughts, emotions, and memories so they can move forward with greater freedom, resilience, and purpose.

Rather than trying to erase or suppress trauma-related experiences, ACT focuses on increasing psychological flexibility, or the ability to stay present, respond effectively to difficult internal experiences, and take meaningful action aligned with personal values. Research suggests ACT can support reductions in trauma-related distress, anxiety, depression, avoidance, and functional impairment, while improving quality of life and emotional well-being.

How ACT Supports Trauma Recovery

ACT can help individuals:

  • Respond differently to intrusive thoughts, memories, or emotional triggers.
  • Reduce avoidance and regain confidence in daily life.
  • Develop grounding and present-moment awareness.
  • Create distance from self-critical or trauma-based beliefs.
  • Reconnect with identity beyond what happened.
  • Clarify values and take steps toward healing, relationships, and growth.
  • Build resilience while making space for understandable pain.

A Compassionate, Trauma-Informed Approach

ACT for trauma is not about “accepting” what happened or minimizing pain. It is about learning how to carry difficult experiences differently so they no longer define or control your life. In a trauma-informed setting, therapy moves at a pace that prioritizes safety, choice, emotional regulation, and collaboration.

Healing after trauma is possible. With support, people can learn new ways of relating to pain while creating a life guided by strength, connection, and purpose.

EMDR is a powerful and effective short term treatment for trauma and psychological distress. It helps transform how your brain processes traumatic experiences, promoting healing and relief. It successfully treats "Big T" traumas (significant, one- time events commonly related to experiences with violence, death, sexual assault, and verbal abuse) and "little T" traumas, including chronic patterns of trauma related to anxiety, eating issues, substance abuse, low self-esteem, and early childhood disruptions with caregivers.

How EMDR Works

EMDR is based on the Accelerated Information Processing (AIP) model, which suggests that trauma can overwhelm normal cognitive and neurological processes, leading to inadequately processed memories stored in isolated networks. Through EMDR, we work to activate the images and thoughts linked to your trauma, integrating this isolated material into broader neural networks. This process allows traumatic memories to be reprocessed, making them less overwhelming and more manageable.

Benefits of EMDR

  • Reprocesses Traumatic Memories: EMDR helps to make distressing memories less intrusive and overwhelming.
  • Promotes Healing: It supports the brain, body, and mind in finding relief from the effects of trauma.
  • Enhances Connection: EMDR fosters a deeper connection with yourself and can improve your relationships with others.

IFS is a compassionate, evidence-informed therapy approach that helps people understand and heal the different “parts” of themselves. Many people notice inner experiences such as a critical voice, an anxious part, a people-pleasing part, or a part that feels overwhelmed. IFS views these responses as protective patterns that developed for understandable reasons—often to help cope with stress, adversity, or painful life experiences.

IFS also emphasizes the presence of the Self - the grounded, wise, compassionate core within each person that can lead to healing and change. Through therapy, individuals learn to relate to their inner parts with curiosity rather than judgment, helping reduce internal conflict and build greater emotional balance.

Emerging research suggests IFS may be helpful for concerns such as trauma symptoms, anxiety, depression, chronic stress, and self-criticism, with studies showing improvements in emotional well-being and functioning for some individuals.

How IFS Helps

IFS therapy can support individuals in:

  • Understanding patterns such as anxiety, perfectionism, people-pleasing, or self-criticism.
  • Healing unresolved wounds that continue to affect present-day life.
  • Reducing shame and developing greater self-compassion.
  • Improving emotional regulation and inner calm.
  • Strengthening confidence, clarity, and decision-making.
  • Building healthier relationships and boundaries.
  • Increasing a sense of wholeness and connection to self.

A Respectful, Non-Pathologizing Approach

IFS does not see people as “broken.” Instead, it recognizes that many symptoms began as protective strategies. Therapy creates a safe, collaborative space to understand these patterns, honour their original purpose, and support healing at a pace that feels manageable.

Healing often begins with understanding yourself differently. IFS offers a compassionate path toward insight, balance, and lasting change.
